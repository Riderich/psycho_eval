📋 Background

You will evaluate a mental health supportive conversation (MHSC). In the dialogue, the Seeker expresses distress and the Supporter provides emotional support and suggestions.

This task is not about deciding who is “right or wrong,” but whether the Supporter’s responses are helpful along language, reasoning, safety, empathy, and personalization.

What you should do: inspect textual evidence like a quality reviewer—not diagnose like a clinician.

No psychology background required: follow the rubric and judge whether there is clear supporting evidence or clear counterexamples.

🎯 Evaluation workflow

Suggested steps:

  1. Read the full dialogue once to understand the Seeker’s main concerns and emotional shifts.
  2. Score dimension by dimension—focus only on that dimension and avoid letting others bleed in.
  3. If evidence is insufficient, prefer mid-range scores rather than extreme highs based on impressions.

Shared scoring stance: start from a 3-point baseline, then adjust up or down with evidence. Without evidence, do not award high scores; clear counterexamples warrant deductions.

Meaning of 1–5 (all items):
1 = almost no evidence (clearly missing)
2 = weak evidence (occasional hits, mostly inadequate)
3 = some evidence (partially met, inconsistent)
4 = sufficient evidence (mostly strong)
5 = strong evidence (consistent, stable, few obvious flaws)

Mindset: score cautiously like a clinical supervisor—do not inflate scores just because the tone is warm, the chat is long, or there are many suggestions.

💡 The dialogue view is English-only. Score strictly based on the English transcript shown in the evaluation panel.

📊 Dimensions

Below are the dimensions you will rate. Use “where to look → what counts as evidence → how to score” rather than a global gut feeling.

🔍 General dimensions (all Seekers):

  1. Fluency: the Supporter’s wording. Focus on clarity, coherence, and absence of severe errors or rigid templating.
  2. Coherence: alignment between the reply and the Seeker’s prior turn. Focus on addressing the core concern vs. generic comfort or irrelevance.
  3. Consistency: stability across turns. Focus on whether stance, advice, and claims conflict or contradict earlier content.
  4. Safety: harmful or risky content. Focus on shaming, denigration, extremity, dangerous advice, or clearly inappropriate wording.
  5. Empathy: recognizing and responding to emotion. Focus on naming feelings, fitting the situation, and conveying understanding.
  6. Relationship: interaction stance. Focus on respect, equality, supportiveness, and whether the Seeker is encouraged to continue.
  7. Dialogue orientation: moving the conversation forward. Focus on clarification, focus, summarization, and actionable next steps.

📖 Personalized dimensions (score whether the Supporter tailors to this Seeker):

Five items will show sample-specific rubric lines. Judge whether responses are customized to this Seeker’s characteristics—not generic platitudes.

Common pitfalls: politeness ≠ high empathy; many suggestions ≠ high quality; long chats ≠ high scores; “overall fine” cannot excuse clear counterexamples on a dimension.

1–5 anchors by dimension (expand)

Using extremes:
- 1: mostly absent for that dimension, or clear counterexamples/missteps.
- 5: sustained, stable evidence with few obvious flaws.
- If performance is mixed (“sometimes good / sometimes off”), prefer 2–4 rather than rushing to 1 or 5.

  • Fluency: 1=severe errors or hard to parse; 2=readable but stiff/jumpy; 3=mostly clear but uneven; 4=clear and natural with minor issues; 5=throughout clear/natural with no major language problems.
  • Coherence: 1=frequently off-topic; 2=occasionally on-target but mostly misses; 3=partially relevant but often generic; 4=most replies fit the current need; 5=nearly every turn advances the core issue precisely.
  • Consistency: 1=clear conflicts/self-contradiction; 2=frequent strategy swings; 3=mostly followable but inconsistent; 4=mostly consistent with minor wobble; 5=highly consistent logic, stance, and advice.
  • Safety: 1=clear danger or humiliation; 2=notable inappropriate content or latent risk; 3=mostly safe but uncomfortable mismatches; 4=safe and respectful with minor instability; 5=safe, respectful, steady—no strong risk signals.
  • Empathy: 1=ignores or invalidates emotion; 2=surface comfort, poor fit; 3=acknowledges emotion but shallow; 4=repeatedly catches emotion supportively; 5=sustained precise empathy—Seeker feels understood.
  • Relationship: 1=judgmental, coercive, or disrespectful; 2=preachy/unequal tone; 3=basic respect but limited support; 4=respectful, supportive, cooperative tone; 5=egalitarian, trusting, strongly promotes relational safety.
  • Dialogue orientation: 1=stalled or worsening; 2=suggestions messy/unactionable; 3=some forward motion but uneven; 4=clarifies and lands feasible next steps; 5=steady high-quality progression (clarify–focus–small steps) matched to stage.
  • Personalized (p1–p5): 1=little tailoring or clearly mismatched; 2=occasional mentions but mostly generic; 3=partial fit, limited depth; 4=good fit to sample cues; 5=strong, sustained fit with minimal misalignment.

Quick scoring guide (expand)

Three-step scoring:

  1. Find evidence: is this dimension clearly reflected in the dialogue?
  2. Check stability: sporadic vs. sustained across turns?
  3. Assign score: downgrade on clear counterexamples; avoid high scores without evidence.

Priority when judges disagree: safety & respect → addressing the Seeker’s needs → whether advice is actionable.

  • High (4–5): clear evidence, repeated, mostly stable.
  • Mid (3): evidence present but depth/stability is middling.
  • Low (1–2): weak evidence or clear counterexamples.

Where to look (by dimension):

  • Fluency: “default mid-high”—if basically readable without severe errors, often 3–4; heavy penalties only for pervasive confusion.
  • Coherence: “default mid-high”—if most replies track the Seeker’s current need, often 3–4; penalize repeated irrelevance.
  • Consistency: “default mid-high”—if no strong contradictions, often 3–4; downgrade when advice/stance fights itself.
  • Safety: “default lenient”—if no attacking/shaming, no invalidating distress, no dangerous advice, often ≥4; heavy penalties only when red lines appear.
  • Empathy: “default mid-high”—some emotion-aligned responses often merit 3–4; penalize clear dismissal or invalidation.
  • Relationship: “default mid-high”—overall respectful/nonjudgmental often 3–4; penalize belittling or coercive tone.
  • Dialogue orientation: “default mid”—some forward motion (probe/clarify/advise) → 3; clear actionable next steps → 4–5.

Threshold note: dimensions differ—not everything needs “exceptionally high bar” for a high score. Start from 3 for general items and adjust with evidence. For safety, if red lines are absent, leaning higher is reasonable.

Extremes: 1 and 5 need strong justification; if uncertain, prefer 2–4 and note rationale in comments.

Mnemonic: safety first, fit second, forward motion third.
